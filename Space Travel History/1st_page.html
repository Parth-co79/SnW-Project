Space Travel History

The Early Dreams and Theoretical Foundations (Before 1900)

Ancient & Medievel Imagination

Long before rockets or satellites existed, humans looked up at the night sky and dreamed of traveling among the stars. In ancient times, many civilizations imagined reaching the heavens through myth and fantasy.

The Greeks told stories of chariots of the gods flying across the sky, while philosophers such as Aristarchus of Samos proposed that the Earth revolved around the Sun, hinting that our world was only one among many. Lucian of Samosata, a Greek satirist of the 2nd century CE, even wrote True History, a humorous but visionary tale of a voyage to the Moon—often regarded as the earliest example of science fiction.

In India, epics like the Ramayana and texts describing vimanas—flying chariots of the gods—showed that people were already imagining machines that could soar between worlds.

In China, early experiments with gunpowder and fireworks gave rise to tales of officials like Wan Hu, who supposedly strapped rockets to a chair in an attempt to reach the sky. Though likely a legend, it reflected humanity’s desire to break free from Earth’s limits.

Renaissance & Early Science (1400s–1700s)

As science began to replace superstition, thinkers started asking scientific questions about space.

Nicolaus Copernicus (1543)

Nicolaus Copernicus (1543) proposed the heliocentric model — the Sun, not Earth, is at the center of the solar system. This changed humanity’s view of its place in the universe — "We weren’t the center of everything."

Galileo Galilei (1609–1632)

Used the telescope to observe the Moon, Jupiter’s moons, and the Milky Way. Proved that celestial bodies are not perfect spheres, but real worlds — maybe even habitable.This inspired the idea that humans might one day visit them.

Johannes Kepler (1571–1630)

Discovered the laws of planetary motion, showing how planets orbit the Sun.Wrote Somnium (“The Dream”) — a story about a journey to the Moon to explain astronomy. It’s often called the first serious science-fiction story. His character used physics, not magic, to describe space travel — a revolutionary idea for its time.

Isaac Newton (1643–1727)

Published Principia Mathematica, explaining gravity and motion.His laws of motion became the foundation for rocket flight centuries later.Newton famously imagined firing a “cannonball fast enough to orbit Earth” — basically describing orbital motion!→ This thought experiment is now known as Newton’s Cannonball.


As centuries turned, the dream of space travel began to intersect with early engineering. In China, gunpowder rockets had already been used for warfare as early as the 13th century. In India during the late 1700s, Tipu Sultan of Mysore improved rocket design by using metal cylinders to contain the explosive charge, creating more powerful and stable projectiles. These innovations inspired the British Congreve rockets and marked the first real step toward controlled rocket propulsion. Although these devices were weapons, they demonstrated that thrust from combustion could move objects great distances—exactly what future spaceflight would require.

Meanwhile, philosophers and inventors in Europe speculated about flying machines and celestial voyages. Some, like John Wilkins in the 1600s, proposed that people could reach the Moon using wings or balloon-like devices. By the 1800s, such ideas found a home in literature, where imagination inspired science. The French novelist Jules Verne, in From the Earth to the Moon (1865), described a group of explorers launched from a massive cannon in Florida—a location that would later resemble NASA’s real-life launch site at Cape Canaveral. Though Verne’s method was scientifically impossible, he calculated trajectories and escape velocities with remarkable insight for his era. His work captured public imagination and directly inspired future pioneers such as Konstantin Tsiolkovsky. Around the same time, the English writer H. G. Wells envisioned anti-gravity technology and extraterrestrial civilizations in novels like The First Men in the Moon (1901) and The War of the Worlds (1898), helping people imagine what space travel might actually feel like.

By the close of the nineteenth century, humanity had learned the fundamental laws of motion and gravity, developed practical rockets for warfare, and begun to visualize voyages beyond Earth through both science and fiction. The stage was set: all that remained was to turn these dreams and theories into real machines capable of escaping Earth’s pull. That transformation would arrive with the dawn of the twentieth century, when visionaries like Konstantin Tsiolkovsky, Robert Goddard, and Hermann Oberth transformed imagination into rocket science—and the dream of spaceflight into reality.
