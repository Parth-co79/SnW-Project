Space Travel History

The Birth of Rocket Science (1900–1945)

At the start of the 20th century, science and technology were advancing rapidly. Theories of motion, gravity, and energy from Newton’s era were now being applied practically. Scientists began to ask if rockets could take humanity beyond Earth, transforming centuries of imagination into engineering.


Konstantin Tsiolkovsky

In 1903, Russian schoolteacher and mathematician Konstantin Tsiolkovsky published “ The exploration of Cosmic Space by Means of response bias. He proved scientifically that rockets could work in the vacuum of space, because they propel themselves by pushing exhaust backward — not by pushing against air. ”

He developed the Tsiolkovsky Rocket Equation, which is the basic mathematical formula used to understand how rockets move and gain speed.Tsiolkovsky proposed using liquid fuel rather than gunpowder in rockets, as it provides better efficiency and more control.He designed early concepts for multi-stage rockets, space stations, airlocks, and space colonies.Even though he never built a real rocket, his theoretical contributions earned him the title of "Father of Astronautics".A famous quote by him is: “Earth is the cradle of humanity, but one cannot live in a cradle forever.”

Robert H. Goddard

Robert Hutchings Goddard, an American physicist and engineer, was the first to build and launch a liquid-fueled rocket.In 1919, he published “A Method of Reaching Extreme Altitudes,” explaining how rockets could reach outer space and even the Moon.He was ridiculed by newspapers for saying rockets could work in space, but he ignored criticism.On March 16, 1926, in Massachusetts, he launched the world’s first liquid-fueled rocket.The rocket flew 12.5 meters high and lasted 2.5 seconds.It was small, but it proved liquid propulsion was possible.Goddard developed gyroscopic stabilization, steerable nozzles, and efficient fuel systems, which are still used in rockets today.His work laid the engineering base for future space exploration.

Hermann Oberth

In 1923, German physicist Hermann Oberth published “The Rocket into Planetary Space.” analyzed how rockets could reach outer space using real physics and engineering designs.Oberth proposed: Multistage rockets for reaching high altitudes.Space suits for astronauts.Reusable spacecraft concepts.He inspired the creation of German Rocket Societies, including the VfR (Verein für Raumschiffahrt) – “Society for Space Travel.” young engineers, including Wernher von Braun, were motivated by Oberth’s work.Oberth became one of the key founders of modern astronautics, along with Tsiolkovsky and Goddard.


The 1920s and 1930s saw a wave of rocket societies form in several countries. In Germany, the Verein für Raumschiffahrt (VfR), or “Society for Space Travel,” built and launched experimental rockets based on Oberth’s designs. In the Soviet Union, engineers inspired by Tsiolkovsky began experimenting with rocket engines and fuel systems under organizations like GIRD (Group for the Study of Reactive Motion). Similar efforts took place in the United States and elsewhere, as inventors and scientists exchanged ideas through journals and exhibitions. For the first time, rocketry became a global scientific pursuit rather than an isolated dream.

However, the onset of World War II (1939–1945) transformed rocket research from scientific curiosity into military technology. Germany, under Nazi leadership, invested heavily in rocket weapons development. Under the direction of Wernher von Braun, engineers at Peenemünde created the V-2 rocket (known as Vergeltungswaffe 2, or “Vengeance Weapon 2”). This was the first long-range ballistic missile in history and the first human-made object to reach the edge of space. Launched in 1944, it traveled at supersonic speeds, reaching altitudes of around 100 kilometers before striking targets in Europe. Although built for war, the V-2 proved that large rockets could leave the atmosphere—an essential milestone for future space exploration.

When the war ended in 1945, both the United States and the Soviet Union recognized the strategic and scientific value of rocket technology. Through Operation Paperclip, the U.S. secretly brought dozens of German scientists, including von Braun, to America, where they continued developing rockets for the U.S. Army. The Soviet Union, too, captured German designs and engineers to accelerate its own rocket program. These efforts laid the groundwork for the Cold War space race that would soon follow.

Thus, by 1945, the age of rocket science had truly begun. What started as a mix of imagination, mathematics, and experimentation had evolved into powerful technology capable of reaching the boundaries of space. The theories of Tsiolkovsky, the experiments of Goddard, and the engineering of Oberth and von Braun together formed the foundation of modern astronautics. Humanity had finally built machines that could pierce the sky—and the next step would be to send them not just above Earth, but into orbit, to the Moon, and beyond.
